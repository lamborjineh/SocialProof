⚠️ Does the content use emotionally charged or biased language?
💡 Watch for: "SHOCKING", "You won't believe", excessive "!!!", absolute words like "always/never/everyone", or appeals to fear/anger.
Skip this step
📊 Does the content provide verifiable evidence for its claims?
💡 Vague references like "experts say" or "studies show" without links are not verifiable evidence. Good evidence is specific and traceable.

🎯 What is the purpose or intent of this content?
💡 Knowing the why behind content helps you evaluate it more critically. Persuasive content isn't necessarily wrong — but it should be read differently than reporting.
Skip this step
👥 Who is the target audience of this content?
💡 Misinformation is often crafted for specific audiences — appealing to their beliefs, fears, or identity. Ask yourself: why would this audience be targeted?
Skip this step
🧠 Does the content use sound logic and reasoning?
💡 Common fallacies: False equivalence (treating unequal things as equal), ad hominem (attacking the person not the argument), slippery slope (assuming one event leads to extreme outcomes without evidence).
Skip this step
🔄 Corroboration — Do other independent sources say the same thing?
👉 This is where a lot of misinformation hides.
Ask yourself: Is this story only on one site? Are opposing views being ignored? What's missing from this account? Legitimate news is usually reported by multiple independent outlets.
💡 Completeness check: Are opposing views ignored? Is context stripped away? A true account should hold up when you read it alongside other reporting.
